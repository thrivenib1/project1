Writing Style Guidelines

GLDES05

Version: 3.00

Date: 17th May 2010

This guideline is only for the writing style and language, and does not refer to content presentation. For content presentation, refer to Guidelines for User Guide

User Documentation such as User Guides, Installation Guides, and Online Help.

Language

Use British English

in all the internal documents

for documents created for European clients

Use American English

for documents created for American clients and clients of countries who use American English such as China, Japan, and Korea

Make the language friendly for non English speaking users. During the localization of software, translators should face no ambiguity in interpretation of any text, and feel comfortable with the language.

Grammar

Mood

Use the imperative mood for describing procedures, troubleshooting and other such tutorial type of text. Ex. Ensure that JRE is installed in the specified path.

Use the indicative mood for describing concepts, functionality, or limitations of the software. Ex. If JRE is not installed in the specified path, or is of a lower version than required, XXXX cannot run properly.

Tense

Do not use present perfect tense needlessly.

Articles

Do not skip the articles

Use 'a' before words pronounced with a consonant sound and 'an' before words with a vowel sound. Ex. an hour, an undertaking, a day, a UNIX session

In case of abbreviations/acronyms, use 'a' or 'an' based on the way the acronym is pronounced and not by the spelling. Ex. an SEPG meeting, an SGML document, a SIM card, a URL

Tone

Do not be aggressive in giving instructions. Do not write sentences such as "You should install PostgreSQL ….". Instead, write "Install PostgreSQL ..."

You must install PostgreSQL before... Instead write PostgreSQL must have been installed before ...

Punctuation

Avoid unnecessary and wrong usage of

Uppercase (capital) for the first letter of a word (unless it is the first word of a sentence) Ex. Ensure that the CD is placed in the Drive. It should be "drive" not "Drive"

All uppercase letters (ALLCAPS) for emphasis (such usage is even considered rude) Ex. DO NOT OPEN THE DRIVE WHILE THE CD IS BEING RECORDED Use other styles such as bold or bold and italic for conveying the importance or emphasis.

bold and italic fonts. If some text is inside single or double quote marks, do not add bold or italic style for emphasis.

bold and/or uppercase letters for menu options at wrong places

Voice

Avoid passive voice sentences to the extent possible. However, do not needlessly complicate a sentence and make it unclear merely to avoid passive voice.

Referencing

Avoid using phrases like "in the figure below" or "in the figure above". Give figure caption or figure number.

Avoid manual references to the figure numbers. Use the Insert Cross Reference option of MS Word to refer to figures and sections.

Refer to the caption of the figure or name of the section if the Insert option is not used.

Give references only for creating better understanding of a concept and not merely for informing the existence of information and confusing the user.

Content

While preparing the initial draft, insert comments / notes wherever screens need to be inserted. Give the correct reference to the name of the dialog box screen to be inserted.

Place Terminology before the text in tutorial kind of manuals.

Place Glossary at the end of the text in reference kind of manuals.
