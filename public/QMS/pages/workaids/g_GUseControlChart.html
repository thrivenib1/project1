Introduction to Control Charts

A control chart is a graphical device to aid in process control. It consists of a graph with a central line denoting the target value or average quality and two limit lines on either side of the central line called ‘Upper Control Limit’ and ‘Lower Control Limit’. Quality measured periodically is plotted on the chart and status of control accessed.

In any process, regardless of how well designed or carefully maintained, variation in quality is an ever-present phenomenon. Such variation may be due to a variety of causes.

These causes are classified into two categories viz.

ASSIGNABLE CAUSES: such as different settings of a machine, different raw material batches, changes in operators, operator errors etc.

CHANCE CAUSES: which are uncontrollable and comprising very small differences in raw materials in the same batch or machines, actual conditions etc.

When no assignable causes of variation are present in a process and it operates only under a constant system of chance cause, the process is said to be in a state of Statistical Control. In that state, the process produces consistent quality. Occasionally, assignable causes enter the process resulting in a change to out-of-control state when process output shows large variation and hence fails to meet requirements.

A major objective of Statistical Quality Control is to quickly detect process shifts due to assignable causes so that corrective action can be taken to prevent non-conforming products being turned out.

A Control Chart facilitates this. Thus a Control Chart is an on-line process control technique widely used for process control. It consist of a graph with a central line denoting the target value and two limit lines on either side of the central line called upper and lower control limits. Quality measured periodically is plotted on the chart and status of control assessed. As long as the plotted point is within the control limits, the process is left alone. However, if a point falls on or below the lower control limit or above the upper control limit, it is an indication of the presence of assignable causes, which need to be eliminated by suitable corrective action.

The control chart was first proposed in 1924 by Dr. Walter A. Shewhart an Engineer in the Bell Telephone Laboratories USA with a view to eliminating abnormal variation in process output by distinguishing variation due to assignable causes from those due to chance causes.

Interpretation of Control Charts

Test for Stability

The following observation in a chart indicates instability and the process is not under control.

·   A single point falls outside the upper or the lower control lines.

·   At least two out of three successive values fall on the same side and more than two sigma units away from the centre line( 3s = UCL – CL).

·   At least four out of five successive values fall on the same side and more than one sigma unit away from the centre line

Recognizing patterns

State of control is revealed when no point exceeds the control limits in the chart and when the points are equally and randomly distributed. A few patterns typically of out of control are given below.

Patterns

1. Points exceeding limits

2 . Run of points on one side of central line

3. Trend

The points form a continuous upward or downward trend. This may be due to some systemic cause such as tool wear.

4. Points too close to the central line

If this is noted on X-Chart, suspect subgroup formation.

 

5. Periodicity

This pattern is not normal, look for some systematic cause affecting the process.

If the points show the same pattern change over equal intervals, it is called ‘Periodicity’.

Interpretation of Attribute Charts

Each chart includes statistically determined upper and lower control limits, indicating the bounds of expected process behaviour. The fluctuation of the points between the control limits is due to the variation that is intrinsic (built in) to the process. We say that this variation is due to "common causes" that influence the process. Any points outside the control limits can be attributed to a "special cause," implying a shift in the process. When a process is influenced by only common causes, then it is stable, and can be predicted. Thus, a key value of the control chart is to identify the occurrence of special causes, so that they can be removed, with a reduction in overall process variation. Then, the process can be further improved by either relocating the process to an optimal average level, or decreasing the variation due to common causes.

Attribute charts are fairly simple to interpret: merely look for out of control points. If there are any, then the special causes must be eliminated. Brainstorm and conduct Designed Experiments to find those process elements that contribute to sporadic changes in process location.

Remove all values that are affected by the assignable causes and recalculate the central, upper and lower control limits. Draw the chart and continue the process till you establish process stability and capability.
