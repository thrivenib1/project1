1.     BRAINSTORMING

Brain storming is a group technique for generating new and useful ideas for finding solutions to problems or even for identifying critical problems itself. An ideal group will be of 5 to 10 people. There should be a facilitator to guide and control the group. It uses a few simple rules for discussion on a subject matter that contributes to originality and innovation.

2.     RULES FOR BRAINSTORMING

·        No criticism or evaluation

·        Try not to be conventional

·        Aim for quantity

·        Look for new ideas

·        Build on ideas

·        Contribute in turn

·        One idea per turn

·        You may pass

·        Do not explain ideas

3.     USES OF BRAINSTORMING

Brainstorming can be used at various stages of problem solving.

It can be used for:

a) Identification of problem.

b) Finding out the possible causes to the problem

c) Finding out the solution

d) Predicting the possible resistance to the solution

e) Finding out the process to overcome the resistance

4.     METHODS OF BRAINSTORMING

Brainstorming may be done in three different ways.

1.      Free wheeling or unstructured

2.      Slip Method

3.      Round Robin or Structured Process

a)     Free wheeling or unstructured.

Here there is no hold, on bar. A department head or a group leader may call his people and explain the subject for Brainstorming and all are expected to come out with his/her ideas. No limit in number at a time. One can reel out even hundred ideas at a stroke.

This is a good system as it is spontaneous, stimulates creativity of the individual and can easily build his/her ideas on others and the continuity helps, in the thinking process.

But there are certain disadvantages in this system. Slow thinking persons and lacking confidence may put shutters to their thinking, seeing the enthusiasm of the strong dominating persons in the group. Since this is free wheeling many may speak simultaneously and invariably confusion prevails. Many times people stray away from the main point.

Freewheeling calls for a tactful and proper handling. The Leader should be a good observer and should have the ability to locate those who do not contribute and encourage them to come out with their ideas.

b)     Slip Method.

Here you ask the individual to put his/her ideas on a slip of paper and give it for consideration. One need not write his/her name. Such anonymity makes people to open up freely. When a group is big and when arranging meeting is difficult, this system can be used.

Eg: CEDAC (Cause Effect Diagram with addition of cards uses such a method.

But the drawback of such a system is high. You cannot build ideas on others’ ideas. Hitch-hiking process is not possible. Some time illegible hand writing or improper presentation leads to problem. Due to anonymity, it is difficult to get things rectified. If anonymity is avoided, then ideas may not flow in. It is a very slow process. It can be done in stages; by collecting ideas and displaying them. Then again for more ideas etc.

c)     Round Robin or Structured Process

In Quality Circle activity we invariably use this method, as development of idea is the primary aim of Quality Circles.

Let us see how this is done.

1.      Gather a group which is willing to work together. In case of Quality Circle we have already such a group.

2.      Select a leader. A leader is there in Quality Circle. He should be open minded, amiable and motivating. He should have control over the group to keep on track and at the same time encourage ideas

3.      Choose a meeting place where there is no disturbance or distraction.

4.      Prior intimation to the group regarding the issue on which they are going to brainstorm is essential. In case such intimation is not possible due to any reason explain it in a clear manner to the group and make sure that it has reached them correctly.

5.      Whenever such intimation is send or explained in person make sure that the statement is specific and clear and at the same time broad enough to include useful ideas. Such note or talk should not create any biased idea or opinion in the minds of the people.

6.      Give the required incubation time for people to think and contribute.

7.      Encourage for more quantity than quality.

8.      Select a person to record the proceedings of the session. Use a flipchart, black board or OHP for this purpose. They should be in full view of the group so that development of ideas over ideas is possible.

9.      It is better sometimes to have guests or invitees that are knowledgeable on the subject. A different way of thinking and fresh ideas helps a lot, but everyone follows the norms setup.

10.   While brainstorming there should not be any criticism or evaluation of ideas.

11.   Think in a radical way and try to contribute and bring out maximum ideas.

12.   Do not hesitate to hitchhike over others’ ideas.

13.   Everyone should contribute in turn and only one idea at a time. If one cannot think of any at a given time he may say ‘Pass’, but will always get another chance in the next round.

14.   Clarify but do not explain or criticize the idea.

15.   You can always do a warm-up session on a neutral subject before taking up the main issue but such sessions should be for a maximum period of 5-10 minutes.

16.   There are various ways of killing an idea. Do not ever permit such things.

17.   Never allow a session to carry on to the fatigue point. If session looses grip you can have diversion or even the session can break. Maximum time for a session is 40-45 minutes. You can break and continue on another day.

18.   A little fun is healthy and interesting. Laugh with someone but at them.

19.   To bring creativity use five W’s and one H (when, where, why, what, who and how).

20.   While processing the idea combine and group similar ideas.

21.   Agree on evaluation criteria.

22.   For a list of problems collect data on the severity.

5.     CATALYSING THE SESSION

If the brainstorm seems to slowdown, the leader may suggest piggy backing. Piggy backing is building on others ideas.

Another technique is to suggest opposites. Members should try to give opposite suggestions to whatever ideas we have about anything. Sometimes that may lead to a good result, or sometimes a combination of "for" and "against" suggestions can give us valuable results. Sometimes we can also try quick associations. The leader gives a word and the members respond as quickly as possible with an associated word, which might apply to the problem in some way. For example, let us say the leader mentions the word ‘advertisement’. Then others might quickly mention TV, Film, Magazine, Posters at Railway Station and Street Junction or even street play.

6.     CAUSE AND EFFECT DIAGRAM

Any defect in a component, a product or service could be due to one or more causes .To find out the relationship between the causes and effect, a diagram is drawn systematically by mapping out all the probable causes influencing the effect. This is called Cause and Effect Diagram.

Generally a brainstorming session is used to generate ideas towards problem solving. Cause & effect Diagram is a tool to organize the ideas collected in a structured way representing the relationship of possible causes of a problem to the problem.

7.     HOW TO USE CAUSE AND EFFECT DIAGRAM

It can be used in the following ways:

·        Preparing itself is an educational process.

·        It can be used as a guide for discussion.

·        You can use it to find out reasons behind the causes in a systematic manner.

·        It can be used to post data for future reference.

·        A detailed cause and effect diagram making exercise helps to have an in-depth knowledge about the process.

·        It can be used for any kind of problem.

8.     USES OF CAUSE AND EFFECT DIAGRAM

·        To investigate and list down the cause and effect relationship of problem under investigation.

·        Analyze the problem to trace the real root cause.

·        To help stratification for collection of further data to confirm relationship.

·        To help evolve counter-measure.

9.     How to Prepare a C E Diagram

·        Clarify the problem

·        Gather members for discussion

·        Conduct Brainstorming session

·        Group the causes into 4M’s (Man, Material, Machine and Method) or as relevant to the particular problem

·        Draw the cause and effect diagram

·        Check for missing information

·        Determine importance of significance of causes

Three types of C E Diagram

a) Dispersion analysis type

b) Process Classification Type

c) Cause Enumeration Type

Dispersion analysis type

In this type how the causes for the effect are disposed or distributed and are shown in sequential manner. By raising the question 'why', cause is established. Sub-cause is fixed to that. To the sub cause, sub-sub cause is connoted. There can be sub-sub-sub cause to the sub-sub-cause.

Process Classification Type

This system follows the process and all things that may affect at various stages of process.

Cause Enumeration Type

The completed diagram may resemble cause dispersion type but this is done by simply listing all possible causes which occur to the mind. Causes subsequently are arranged in accordance to the product quality showing the relationship between the cause and effect. The advantage of this type is all causes are listed and thus no major cause is missed.

The disadvantage is that it may be difficult to relate the twig causes to the result, making the diagram difficult to draw.

Procedure for making cause and effect diagram

Step: 1

Put the effect in a square box on the right hand side of a paper. Draw a thick arrow line from left to right touching the box. The effect put in the box should be very specific. If the effect put in the box is general, then the causes would also be general and the analysis would lead us nowhere. General problem statement such as poor quality, poor working condition, too much paper work etc. should be avoided.

Step: 2

Draw slanting arrows touching the main arrow to indicate four, five or six major causes. Enclose the main causes in rectangular boxes. The main causes could be METHODS, MACHINE, ENVIRONMENT, TOOLS, EQUIPMENTS and HUMAN etc. Generally five or six main causes could be sufficient to cover any problem analysis. In some complex problems more main causes may exist.

Step: 3

Take one main cause and Brain storm for the sub causes relating to the main cause and list them out. Continue the process by taking one main cause after another and reflect on them. Later logically draw the arrows on the slanting arrows touching them to fit in the sub causes. Please see that sub causes which are associated are arranged in proper manner as sub cause and sub cause.

Step: 4

Analyze the causes one by one to find out the extent to which they influence the effect. Eliminate the causes which do not influence and narrow down to a cause or causes which really influence the effect.

10. How to Use Cause and Effect Diagram

On the basis of data, assign an importance to each factor objectively. Based on our experience, knowledge and skill we should try to examine each factor. Generally problems which can be solved on a routine basis in a process would have been already managed. Generally the persistent problems are those which are beyond such measures. Hence using data has got an important purpose.

No effective and useful cause and effect diagram can be made at one step. Basically they have a very great use as a diagram to give us a deep insight on the issues under scrutiny. Hence we should not consider the diagram making as one time exercise. We should understand that they serve the purpose of indicating the areas which need checks, additions and alterations. Repeated attempts to modify the diagram help us to understand things better.

11. How to Interpret Cause and Effect Diagram

Cause and effect diagram is a problem defining tool. Use it effectively to organize the various possible causes known to the group for the effect in an orderly and sequential manner. This will help us to have a clarity regarding the process and the factors which can and are affecting them.

When the cause and effect diagram is made we won’t be having a clear cut idea about the real culprit cause or causes. They can be found out only on collection of data or by discussion based on our experience.

Cause and effect diagram is an aid to present the information in a systematic manner. They by themselves do not indicate the culprit causes to us. This is a step in the systematic approach for solving the problem. We have to take the help of other techniques in identifying the root causes.

Normally main causes will have three or four sub-causes. If it is less than that we can safely assume that further study is needed, to have a better understanding of the problem.

You may take help of 'potential members', supervisors, department head and any other person who has got knowledge and experience in that area.

But for arranging any cause as a main cause we should follow certain procedure. Basically they are to be important and relevant and should satisfy certain conditions such as :

·        We can find a logical relation step through the sub-sub-sub causes to the final effect.

·        It is controllable.

·        In case we eliminate or reduce the intensity of it, corresponding result should be observable in the effect.

12. PARETO CHARTS

Pareto analysis is a technique of arranging data according to priority or importance and using it into a problem solving frame work.

This helps you to find out the "VITAL FEW" from "USEFUL MANY" for problem selection

A Pareto Chart is a vertical bar graph showing problems in a prioritized order, so it can be determined which problems should be tackled first. It is often useful to make Pareto Charts of data collected using the Check Sheet method, and in fact, the Check Sheet option includes a built-in Pareto Charting feature.

Choose the categories (or "problem areas") to collect data for by brainstorming, or use existing data to look for these problem areas. The data you analyze must be "counts" (Attributes data) or costs, and must be additive. Data should as yields or percentages cannot be added, so are inappropriate for Pareto analysis. You should also decide the time period over which the data should be collected.

13. When to Use a Pareto Chart

Pareto charts are typically used to prioritize competing or conflicting "problems," so that resources are allocated to the most significant areas. In general, though, they can be used to determine which of several classifications have the most "count" or cost associated with them. For instance, the number of people using the various ATM's vs. each of the indoor teller locations, or the profit generated from each of twenty product lines. The important limitations are that the data must be in terms of either counts or costs. The data can not be in terms that can't be added, such as percent yields or error rates.

Only Attributes data can be applied to a Pareto Chart.

14. Interpreting a Pareto Chart

The left vertical axis of the Pareto has "counts" or "cost" depending on the data used. Each vertical bar represents the contribution to the total from a given "problem" area. The bars are placed on the graph in rank order that is the bar at the left has the highest contribution to counts or cost. The right vertical axis has percent demarcations. A cumulative line is used to add the percentages from each bar, starting at the left (highest cost or count) bar. Thus, we can see which bars contribute the most problems, and with the cumulative line, determine how much of the total problem will be fixed by addressing the highest few.

Remember that the purpose of the Pareto is to distinguish the "vital few from the trivial many." Therefore, we would like only a few bars on the left side of the Pareto that account for most, say 80%, of the problems. Then it's clear which areas we should address. We can also look at the cumulative line to tell us if our Pareto is working well. The cumulative line should be steep, with a lot of arch to it, implying that the first few problem areas rapidly add to a high percentage of the total problems. If the cumulative line is straight, it is telling us that the contribution from each successive bar (after the first) is about even. These bars, then, should be about the same height. This says that no problems stand out as being more bothersome than the rest, which doesn't help much for problem solving.

We can get "flat" Pareto Diagrams just by the way we gather our data. If we separate major problem areas into many small problem areas, than each bar won't have much in it, hence a flat shape. We might consider regrouping the problems into meaningful, larger problem areas.

15. SCATTER DIAGRAMS

A Scatter Diagram examines the relationships between data collected for two different characteristics. Although the Scatter Diagram cannot determine the cause of such a relationship, it can show whether or not such a relationship exists, and if so, just how strong it is. The analysis produced by the Scatter Diagram is called Regression Analysis.

16. When to Use a Scatter Diagram

Use a Scatter Diagram to determine if there is correlation between two characteristics. Correlation implies that as one variable changes, the other also changes. Although this may indicate a cause and effect relationship, this is not always the case, since there may be a third characteristic (or many more) that are actually the cause, and both the characteristics of interest are the effect.

Sometimes, though, if we know that there is good correlation between two characteristics, we can use one to predict the other, particularly if one characteristic is easy to measure and the other isn't. For instance, if we prove that weight gain in the first trimester of pregnancy correlates well with fetus development, we can use weight gain as a predictor. The alternative would be expensive tests to monitor the actual development of the fetus.

17. Interpreting a Scatter Diagram

The x-axis in the diagram is used to measure the scale of one characteristic (called the independent variable), and the y-axis measures the second, (called the dependent variable). If the two characteristics are somehow related, the pattern formed by plotting them in a Scatter Diagram will show clustering in a certain direction and tightness. The more the cluster approaches a line in appearance, the more the two characteristics are likely to be linearly correlated.

The relative correlation of one characteristic to another can be seen both from how closely points cluster the line, and the correlation coefficient in the Statistics window. Values near one imply very high correlation between the characteristics, meaning that a change in one characteristic will be accompanied by change in the other characteristic. Positive correlation means that as one increase, so does the other, and is shown on the Scatter Diagram as a line with positive slope. Negative correlation implies that as one characteristic increases, the other decreases, and a negative slope is seen on the Scatter Diagram.

Remember that correlation does not necessarily mean a cause and effect relationship exists. Both the characteristics may be the effect of a number of other causes. All it means is that there appears to be a relationship between the two over the range of the data. Be careful not to extrapolate beyond the data region, since you have no experience upon which to draw.

The confidence lines indicate the bounds of variation that can be expected for the fitted Regression function. The width of the Confidence Interval provides an indication of the quality of the fitted Regression function. The fact that the confidence lines diverge at the ends, and converge in the middle, may be explained one of two ways:

The regression function, in this case a line, requires estimation of two parameters: slope and y-intercept. The error in estimating slope can be visualized by imagining the fitted line's slope varying about its middle. This results in the hourglass-shaped region shown by the confidence intervals.

The center of the data is located near the middle of the fitted line. The ability to predict the regression function should be better where there is more data; hence the confidence limits are narrower at the middle.

HISTOGRAM

Histogram is a graphical representation of a frequency distribution which is a summary of variation in a product or process.

When data are collected, it is viewed as a sample of all possible observations from a process. The totality of all possible observations we can think of is called population. This few observations taken is called sample.

The first step in summarizing the data as above is to divide the range of variation into suitable number of groups and classify the observed values in those groups. Such a table where the frequencies of the values are tabulated is called frequency table which can be represented pictorially in the form of a bar graph. This bar graph of a frequency table is called HISTOGRAM.

INTERPRETATION OF HISTOGRAM

Histogram shows the pattern of variation. By looking at the Histogram, information on the following can be obtained.

a) Location of mean value i.e. process level

b) Extent of variation or spread in the process.

c) Pattern of variation given by the shape of the Histogram-Symmetrical or skew

d) Conformance to prescribed specifications.

It is comparatively easy to infer about (a), (b) and (d); some elaboration is required for (c).

Inferences on Patterns of Variation

A histogram can exhibit a variety of shapes. Clues on the status of control and influence of assigned causes can be got by examination of shape of the histogram. A few of these are presented below.

Symmetrical

Symmetrical or bell shaped. Many quality measurements under conditions of control, give rise to this pattern. Thus, status of control can be inferred in a given case.

The distribution has, a single peak corresponding to the mean or process level. As one moves from the center or mean on either side frequencies fall gradually.

Asymmetrical Skewed Positive

Asymmetrical with more observations to the right of mean.

Asymmetrical Skewed Negative

Asymmetrical with more observations to the left of mean.

Cliff like Histogram

This is a truncated type. Frequencies drop abruptly on the left. This may arise when the components below lower specification limits are removed and is re-inspected. Similarly, truncation can be on right side (corresponding to upper specification limit)

Double Peaked

This pattern arises from a mixture of two conditions-two suppliers of same components, two developers or from different environments. Separate histogram for each condition has to be made.

Plateau Type

This is a mixture of several process conditions. Output of several days mixed together in a lot.

Isolated Peak

It is a small group of data from a different lot / process conditions. Shows that means or process levels are different and variation is different.

CONTROL CHARTS

A control chart is a graphical device to aid in process control. It consists of a graph with a central line denoting the target value or average quality and two limit lines on either side of the central line called ‘Upper Control Limit’ and ‘Lower Control Limit’. Quality measured periodically is plotted on the chart and status of control accessed.

In any process, regardless of now well designed or carefully maintained, variation in quality is an ever present phenomenon. Such variation may be due to a variety of causes. These causes are classified into two categories viz.

a) ASSIGNABLE CAUSES: such as different settings of a machine, different raw material batches, changes in operators, operator errors etc.

b) CHANCE CAUSES: which are uncontrollable and comprising very small differences in raw materials in the same batch or machines, actual conditions etc.

When no assignable causes of variation are present in a process and it operates only under a constant system of chance cause, the process is said to be in a state of Statistical Control. In that state, the process produces consistent quality. Occasionally, assignable causes enter the process resulting in a change to out-of-control state when process output shows large variation and hence fails to meet requirements.

A major objective of Statistical Quality Control is to quickly detect process shifts due to assignable causes so that corrective action can be taken to prevent non-conforming products being turned out.

This is facilitated by a Control Chart. Thus a Control Chart is an on-line process control technique widely used for process control. It consist of a graph with a central line denoting the target value and two limit lines on either side of the central line called upper and lower control limits. Quality measured periodically is plotted on the chart and status of control assessed. As long as the plotted point is within the control limits, the process is left alone. However, if a point falls on or below the lower control limit or above the upper control limit, it is an indication of the presence of assignable causes which need to be eliminated by suitable corrective action.

We shall be using U-charts for attribute data and XmR chart for variable data, collected from the various software development activities.

The details regarding the creation of U-charts and XmR are described in the "Guideline for Control Charts", GLMAA02.
